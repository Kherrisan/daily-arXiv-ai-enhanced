### [1] [Quantum-Classical Hybrid Encryption Framework Based on Simulated BB84 and AES-256: Design and Experimental Evaluation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.02836)
*Hector E Mozo*

Main category: cs.CR

TL;DR: A hybrid encryption framework combines BB84 quantum key distribution with AES-256, featuring HMAC and post-quantum signatures. The Python-based system demonstrates resilience against attacks, offering a practical foundation for quantum-aware cybersecurity.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Traditional cryptographic systems may become vulnerable to quantum adversaries. This work combines quantum and classical methods to establish a secure, forward-compatible encryption framework robust against future quantum threats.

Method: The system integrates simulated BB84 protocol for quantum key generation with AES-256 for data encryption. Modular Python components implement quantum key exchange, encryption layer, secure packaging, HMAC verification, and optional Dilithium-based post-quantum signatures. Architectural resiliency is validated through visual testing of attack scenarios.

Result: Experimental evaluation demonstrates resistance to key tampering, HMAC bypass attempts, and file corruption. Performance metrics show practical feasibility of quantum-classical hybrid approach with acceptable computational overhead.

Conclusion: This work establishes a practical implementation blueprint for quantum-safe systems, proving the viability of hybrid quantum-classical architectures before widespread quantum computing adoption. The modular design enables future upgrades as post-quantum standards mature.

Abstract: This paper presents the design, implementation, and evaluation of a hybrid
encryption framework that combines quantum key distribution, specifically a
simulated BB84 protocol, with AES-256 encryption. The system enables secure
file encryption by leveraging quantum principles for key generation and
classical cryptography for data protection. It introduces integrity validation
mechanisms, including HMAC verification and optional post-quantum digital
signatures, ensuring robustness even in the presence of quantum-capable
adversaries. The entire architecture is implemented in Python, with modular
components simulating quantum key exchange, encryption, and secure packaging.
Experimental results include visual testing of various attack scenarios, such
as key tampering, HMAC failure, and file corruption, demonstrating the
effectiveness and resilience of the approach. The proposed solution serves as a
practical foundation for quantum-aware cybersecurity systems.

### [2] [AI Agents with Decentralized Identifiers and Verifiable Credentials](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.02841)
*Sandro Rodriguez Garzon,Awid Vaziry,Enis Mert Kuzu,Dennis Enrique Gehrmann,Buse Varkan,Alexander Gaballa,Axel Küpper*

Main category: cs.CR

TL;DR: LLM-based agents face challenges in establishing automated trust in multi-agent, cross-domain interactions. The paper introduces a framework using self-sovereign digital identities, DIDs, and VCs to address these issues, though limitations in LLM-controlled security are identified.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Agents operating across environments need autonomous and interoperable trust mechanisms, which current LLM-based agents lack.

Method: The framework assigns agents self-sovereign digital identities with DIDs and VCs, allowing authentication and trust establishment through identity-bound attestations from trusted parties for cross-domain use.

Result: The prototype shows technical feasibility of the approach but highlights risks when LLMs alone manage security procedures.

Conclusion: Digital identities with DIDs and VCs can enable automated trust in multi-agent systems, but LLMs' control over security processes remains a significant limitation.

Abstract: LLM-based AI agents still lack the technical means to automatically build
nuanced and differentiated trust in other agents at the beginning of an
agent-to-agent dialogue. But autonomous and interoperable trust establishing
becomes a fundamental prerequisite once agents start to operate beyond isolated
environments and engage in dialogues across individual or organizational
boundaries. A promising way to fill this gap in Agentic AI is to equip agents
with long-lived digital identities and introduce tamper-proof and flexible
identity-bound attestations of agents, provisioned by commonly trusted third
parties and designed for cross-domain verifiability. This article presents a
conceptual framework and a prototypical multi-agent system, where each agent is
endowed with a self-sovereign digital identity. It combines a unique and
ledger-anchored Decentralized Identifier (DID) of an agent with a set of
third-party issued Verifiable Credentials (VCs). This enables agents at the
start of a dialog to prove ownership of their self-controlled DIDs for
authentication purposes and to establish various cross-domain trust
relationships through the spontaneous exchange of their self-hosted DID-bound
VCs. A comprehensive evaluation of the prototypical implementation demonstrates
technical feasibility but also reveals limitations once an agent's LLM is in
sole charge to control the respective security procedures.

  <summary>Details</summary>

energy inefficiency. Existing consensus protocols such as Proof-of-Work (PoW)

centralization. This paper proposes \textit{Proof-of-Spiking-Neurons (PoSN)}, a
neuromorphic consensus protocol inspired by spiking neural networks. PoSN
encodes transactions as spike trains, elects leaders through competitive firing
dynamics, and finalizes blocks via neural synchronization, enabling parallel
and event-driven consensus with minimal energy overhead. A hybrid system
architecture is implemented on neuromorphic platforms, supported by simulation
frameworks such as Nengo and PyNN. Experimental results show significant gains
in energy efficiency, throughput, and convergence compared to PoB and PoR. PoSN
establishes a foundation for sustainable, adaptive blockchains suitable for
IoT, edge, and large-scale distributed systems.

### [5] [Lightweight Session-Key Rekeying Framework for Secure IoT-Edge Communication](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.02924)
*Haranath Rakshit,Rajkumar Bhandari,Subhasis Banerjee*

Main category: cs.CR

TL;DR: DSEKP is a lightweight, symmetric session-key rekeying protocol that enhances IoT security by providing forward secrecy and replay protection with minimal overhead, implemented and tested on ESP32 and Raspberry Pi 5 with promising results against a static PSK baseline.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The increasing use of IoT networks requires security solutions that are efficient for constrained devices without the heavy computational cost of public-key cryptography. Traditional PSK is vulnerable because of static keys, lack of forward secrecy, and susceptibility to replay attacks.

Method: The paper introduces DSEKP, a lightweight framework that extends PSK into a session-aware system. It uses HKDF-SHA256 to derive dynamic AES-GCM session keys and HMAC for session authentication, implemented over MQTT with ESP32 and Raspberry Pi 5.

Result: DSEKP achieved comparable throughput and reliability to static PSK, with moderate increases in latency (27%) and payload size (10%). It enabled forward secrecy and replay protection while maintaining low computational and bandwidth costs.

Conclusion: DSEKP demonstrates that dynamic symmetric rekeying can significantly improve IoT-Edge security with minimal overhead, offering a practical and scalable solution for enhancing traditional PSK-based systems in constrained IoT environments.

Abstract: The proliferation of Internet of Things (IoT) networks demands security
mechanisms that protect constrained devices without the computational cost of
public-key cryptography. Conventional Pre-Shared Key (PSK) encryption, while
efficient, remains vulnerable due to static key reuse, replay attacks, and the
lack of forward secrecy. This paper presents the Dynamic Session Enhanced Key
Protocol (DSEKP) - a lightweight session-key rekeying framework, a fully
symmetric extension to PSK that derives per-session AES-GCM keys using the
HMAC-based Key Derivation Function (HKDF-SHA256) and authenticates session
establishment through an HMAC proof in a single init-ack exchange. DSEKP was
implemented on an ESP32 IoT sensor node and a Raspberry Pi 5 edge server
communicating through a Mosquitto MQTT broker, and benchmarked against a static
PSK baseline over more than 6,500 encrypted packets per configuration. The
results demonstrate nearly identical throughput and reliability, with moderate
overhead - mean latency increased by 27% and payload size by 10% - while
delivering per-session forward secrecy and built-in replay protection. These
findings confirm that dynamic symmetric rekeying can substantially strengthen
IoT-Edge links with minimal computational and bandwidth cost, offering a
practical migration path from static PSK to session-aware, scalable, and
reproducible IoT security.

### [6] [SELF-REDRAFT: Eliciting Intrinsic Exploration-Exploitation Balance in Test-Time Scaling for Code Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.02854)
*Yixiang Chen,Tianshi Zheng,Shijue Huang,Zhitao He,Yi R. Fung*

Main category: cs.SE

TL;DR: SELF-REDRAFT improves test-time scaling for code generation by enhancing the balance between exploitation and exploration, showing better results than Self-Refine but revealing limitations in feedback generation and discriminative judgment.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Addressing the need for test-time scaling without interpreter feedback in real-world scenarios where test cases are scarce.

Method: Introduction of SELF-REDRAFT, a framework building on Self-Refine to encourage proposing new drafts for solutions with fundamental flaws.

Result: Consistently better performance than Self-Refine under the same iteration limits, but limitations persist due to weak self-generated feedback and discriminative capabilities.

Conclusion: Highlights the importance of feedback and discrimination in LLM-based test-time scaling and shows varying strategies across models.

Abstract: Test-time scaling without interpreter feedback is essential for real-world
code generation scenarios where test cases are not readily available. While
existing paradigms often rely on either greedy exploitation (i.e., iterative
refinement) or stochastic exploration (i.e., relying on sample-based voting or
reranking mechanisms), the balance between these two dimensions remains
underexplored. To investigate the LLM's intrinsic ability to balance
exploitation and exploration, we introduce SELF-REDRAFT, a framework built upon
Self-Refine that encourages the model to propose new drafts for solutions that
are fundamentally flawed. Our results show that SELF-REDRAFT consistently
achieves better performance than Self-Refine when converged under the same
maximum number of iterations. Still, we observe that significant room for
improvement remains, largely due to two core aspects of current self-redraft
capabilities: constrained capacity for generating instructive feedback and
fragile discriminative judgment. We also find that balancing strategies vary
notably across different LLMs, reflecting distinct, model-specific behaviors.
Overall, our study establishes a baseline for intrinsic
exploration-exploitation balancing in test-time scaling and identifies feedback
and discrimination as key areas with potential for future advances.

<details>

Motivation: IT projects' rapid evolution necessitated a shift from traditional methods like waterfall to agile and hybrid models. This study aims to understand the evolution, challenges, and success of these approaches.

Method: Systematic literature review guided by the PRISMA protocol, analyzing peer-reviewed research over the past 8 years.

Result: Hybrid models emerged to address agile limitations in large-scale and regulated settings, balancing flexibility and structure. Critical success factors include leadership support, tailored integration, and continuous improvement.

Conclusion: Contextual adaptation is crucial, and rigid frameworks may hinder success. Organizations should prioritize hybrid models and invest in leadership, process customization, and improvement practices.

Abstract: The rapid evolution of IT projects has driven the transformation of project
management methodologies, from traditional waterfall approaches to agile
frameworks and, more recently, hybrid models. This systematic literature review
investigates the evolution of agile methodologies into hybrid frameworks,
analysing their implementation challenges and success factors. We identify key
trends through PRISMA-guided analysis of peer-reviewed studies from the last 8
years. Hybrid methodologies emerge from agile limitations in large-scale and
regulated environments, combining iterative flexibility with structured
governance. Agile has several implementation challenges, leading to hybrid
methods, and the success hinges on leadership support, tailored process
integration, and continuous improvement mechanisms. The study explores the need
for contextual adaptation over rigid frameworks, offering practical insights
for organisations navigating hybrid transitions.
