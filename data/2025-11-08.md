### [1] [Temporal Analysis Framework for Intrusion Detection Systems: A Novel Taxonomy for Time-Aware Cybersecurity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.03799)
*Tatiana S. Parlanti,Carlos A. Catania*

Main category: cs.CR

TL;DR: This paper proposes a temporal analysis framework and taxonomy for time-aware network intrusion detection, highlighting the limitations of current systems in early detection and the biases in datasets used for research.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The motivation of the paper is to address the gap in current intrusion detection systems (IDS) that primarily detect attacks at later stages, but not the early indicators of compromise. By proposing a new temporal analysis framework, the authors aim to enable earlier detection and transition towards a more proactive cyber defense paradigm.

Method: The method involves a systematic review of over 40 studies published between 2020 and 2025, leading to the creation of a taxonomy that classifies NIDS methods based on their treatment of time, including static per-flow analysis and multi-window sequential modeling.

Result: The results of the review indicate that inter-flow sequential and temporal window-based methods are more effective in covering multiple stages of the MITRE ATT&CK framework from Reconnaissance to Impact. Additionally, the analysis highlights the limitations of existing datasets in emphasizing late-stage attacks.

Conclusion: The conclusion is that the proposed framework represents essential groundwork for developing IDS that can anticipate cyber threats and implement proactive defense strategies, pushing the field toward improved early detection methods.

Abstract: Most intrusion detection systems still identify attacks only after
significant damage has occurred, detecting late-stage tactics rather than early
indicators of compromise. This paper introduces a temporal analysis framework
and taxonomy for time-aware network intrusion detection. Through a systematic
review of over 40 studies published between 2020 and 2025, we classify NIDS
methods according to their treatment of time, from static per-flow analysis to
multi-window sequential modeling. The proposed taxonomy reveals that inter-flow
sequential and temporal window-based methods provide the broadest temporal
coverage across MITRE ATT&CK tactics, enabling detection from Reconnaissance
through Impact stages. Our analysis further exposes systematic bias in widely
used datasets, which emphasize late-stage attacks and thus limit progress
toward early detection. This framework provides essential groundwork for
developing IDS capable of anticipating rather than merely reacting to cyber
threats, advancing the field toward truly proactive defense mechanisms.

### [3] [Security Analysis of Agentic AI Communication Protocols: A Comparative Evaluation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.03841)
*Yedidel Louck,Ariel Stulman,Amit Dvir*

Main category: cs.CR

TL;DR: analyze the structure of a research paper abstract.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: researchers are interested in improving the security of multi-agent system communication protocols, as current implementations are insufficiently secure and vulnerabilities are prevalent in both CORAL and ACP.

Method: empirical, comparative security analysis of two implementations against a literature-based evaluation, using a 14 point vulnerability taxonomy to assess defenses in key areas (authentication, authorization, integrity, confidentiality, availability).

Abstract: Multi-agent systems (MAS) powered by artificial intelligence (AI) are
increasingly foundational to complex, distributed workflows. Yet, the security
of their underlying communication protocols remains critically under-examined.
This paper presents the first empirical, comparative security analysis of the
official CORAL implementation and a high-fidelity, SDK-based ACP
implementation, benchmarked against a literature-based evaluation of A2A. Using
a 14 point vulnerability taxonomy, we systematically assess their defenses
across authentication, authorization, integrity, confidentiality, and
availability. Our results reveal a pronounced security dichotomy: CORAL
exhibits a robust architectural design, particularly in its transport-layer
message validation and session isolation, but suffers from critical
implementation-level vulnerabilities, including authentication and
authorization failures at its SSE gateway. Conversely, ACP's architectural
flexibility, most notably its optional JWS enforcement, translates into
high-impact integrity and confidentiality flaws. We contextualize these
findings within current industry trends, highlighting that existing protocols
remain insufficiently secure. As a path forward, we recommend a hybrid approach
that combines CORAL's integrated architecture with ACP's mandatory per-message
integrity guarantees, laying the groundwork for resilient, next-generation
agent communications.

### [4] [Secure Code Generation at Scale with Reflexion](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.03898)
*Arup Datta,Ahmed Aljohani,Hyunsook Do*

Main category: cs.CR

TL;DR: This paper evaluates secure code generation of instruction-tuned code LLMs using Instruct Prime and reflexion prompting, finding that models can improve security via iterative prompting with diverse results across languages and CWE types.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Large language models (LLMs) are commonly used to generate code, but code functionality doesn't always imply security. This study seeks to address the lack of secure code generation by evaluating different methods (Instruct Primes vs. baseline) and prompting strategies (reflexion prompting) in code LLMs.

Method: The research uses Instruct Prime (with no compliance-required prompts or cue contamination) to evaluate five instruction-tuned code LLMs with ICD, comparing a zero-shot baseline against a three-round reflexion prompting approach. Security metrics are measured through Repair, Regression, and NetGain, considering language and CWE family.

Result: 25-33% of initial programs had security issues at the zero-shot baseline. Weak cryptography and config-dependent bugs were hardest to avoid. Python had the highest secure rates (79.43% after t3) while C/C# were lowest. Reflexion prompting improved average accuracy from 70.74% to 79.43% with diminishing returns after two rounds.

Conclusion: The results show that iterative prompting through reflexion significantly improves LLM-generated code security, but tasks requiring in-depth reasoning remain challenging. This highlights the need for better training data and model evaluation in security contexts specifically.

Abstract: Large language models (LLMs) are now widely used to draft and refactor code,
but code that works is not necessarily secure. We evaluate secure code
generation using the Instruct Prime, which eliminated compliance-required
prompts and cue contamination, and evaluate five instruction-tuned code LLMs
using a zero-shot baseline and a three-round reflexion prompting approach.
Security is measured using the Insecure Code Detector (ICD), and results are
reported by measuring Repair, Regression, and NetGain metrics, considering the
programming language and CWE family. Our findings show that insecurity remains
common at the first round: roughly 25-33% of programs are insecure at a
zero-shot baseline (t0 ). Weak cryptography/config-dependent bugs are the
hardest to avoid while templated ones like XSS, code injection, and hard-coded
secrets are handled more reliably. Python yields the highest secure rates; C
and C# are the lowest, with Java, JS, PHP, and C++ in the middle. Reflexion
prompting improves security for all models, improving average accuracy from
70.74% at t0 to 79.43% at t3 , with the largest gains in the first round
followed by diminishing returns. The trends with Repair, Regression, and
NetGain metrics show that applying one to two rounds produces most of the
benefits. A replication package is available at
https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.17065846.

*Victor Mattos,João Henrique Schmidt,Amit Bhaya,Alan Oliveira de Sá,Daniel Sadoc Menasché,Gaurav Srivastava*

Main category: cs.CR

TL;DR: This paper explores the modeling and assessment of covert man-in-the-middle attacks in critical infrastructures, particularly water treatment facilities, and evaluates the effectiveness of anomaly detection methods against these attacks.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Cyberattacks on critical infrastructures pose serious risks to public health, safety, and the environment, making it essential to develop and evaluate effective security measures.

Method: The study uses a second-order linear time-invariant with time delay model to simulate water treatment dynamics, designs covert attacks using system identification techniques, and evaluates the PASAD anomaly detection method as a countermeasure.

Result: The results show that system noise and inaccuracies in the attacker's plant model significantly affect the stealthiness of attacks, indicating vulnerabilities in current detection strategies.

Conclusion: The need for more robust detection strategies in industrial control environments is underscored by the findings, which emphasize the impact of system noise and model inaccuracies on attack stealthiness.

Abstract: Cyberattacks targeting critical infrastructures, such as water treatment
facilities, represent significant threats to public health, safety, and the
environment. This paper introduces a systematic approach for modeling and
assessing covert man-in-the-middle (MitM) attacks that leverage system
identification techniques to inform the attack design. We focus on the
attacker's ability to deploy a covert controller, and we evaluate
countermeasures based on the Process-Aware Stealthy Attack Detection (PASAD)
anomaly detection method. Using a second-order linear time-invariant with time
delay model, representative of water treatment dynamics, we design and simulate
stealthy attacks. Our results highlight how factors such as system noise and
inaccuracies in the attacker's plant model influence the attack's stealthiness,
underscoring the need for more robust detection strategies in industrial
control environments.

### [6] [Tutorial Debriefing: Applied Statistical Causal Inference in Requirements Engineering](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.03875)
*Julian Frattini,Hans-Martin Heyn,Robert Feldt,Richard Torkar*

Main category: cs.SE

TL;DR: The paper discusses the importance of transferring SE research knowledge into practice and proposes the use of statistical causal inference for situations where randomized control trials are not feasible.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The paper is motivated by the need to ensure that SE research contributes to the betterment of software producers and consumers by effectively transferring research knowledge into practical applications.

Method: The paper advocates for the use of statistical causal inference methods when conducting controlled experiments is not feasible, suggesting it as a reliable alternative.

Result: The paper highlights that applying tools, processes, and guidelines derived from SE research can improve development processes and user experiences, but the effectiveness requires evidence of causal relationships.

Conclusion: Conclusion emphasizes the necessity of using statistical causal inference as a valid method to establish causal relationships in the absence of randomized control trials in SE research.

Abstract: As any scientific discipline, the software engineering (SE) research
community strives to contribute to the betterment of the target population of
our research: software producers and consumers. We will only achieve this
betterment if we manage to transfer the knowledge acquired during research into
practice. This transferal of knowledge may come in the form of tools,
processes, and guidelines for software developers. However, the value of these
contributions hinges on the assumption that applying them causes an improvement
of the development process, user experience, or other performance metrics. Such
a promise requires evidence of causal relationships between an exposure or
intervention (i.e., the contributed tool, process or guideline) and an outcome
(i.e., performance metrics). A straight-forward approach to obtaining this
evidence is via controlled experiments in which a sample of a population is
randomly divided into a group exposed to the new tool, process, or guideline,
and a control group. However, such randomized control trials may not be
legally, ethically, or logistically feasible. In these cases, we need a
reliable process for statistical causal inference (SCI) from observational
data.

### [7] [Collaborative Agents for Automated Program Repair in Ruby](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.03925)
*Nikta Akbarpour,Mahdieh Sadat Benis,Fatemeh Hendijani Fard,Ali Ouni,Mohamed Aymen Saied*

Main category: cs.SE

TL;DR: The paper introduces RAMP, a lightweight framework for debugging Ruby code with LLM, which is evaluated on the XCodeEval benchmark.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Despite the progress in using LLMs for program repair, existing methods are computationally expensive and mostly limited to a few languages. Ruby, though widely used in web development, has been largely neglected in APR research.

Method: RAMP formulates program repair as a feedback-driven, iterative process using a team of collaborative agents. These agents generates targeted tests, reflect on errors, and refine candidate fixes. The framework is lightweight, and does not rely on large multilingual repair databases or expensive fine-tuning.

Result: RAMP achieves a 67% pass@1 on the XCodeEval benchmark, surpassing previous methods. It converges quickly in five iterations, with ablation studies showing that test generation and self-reflection are keys to its success. RAMP is effective for repairing wrong answers, compilation errors, and runtime errors.

Conclusion: RAMP provides new insights into multi-agent program repair and establishes a foundation for extending LLM-based debugging tools for under-studied languages.

Abstract: Automated Program Repair (APR) has advanced rapidly with Large Language
Models (LLMs), but most existing methods remain computationally expensive, and
focused on a small set of languages. Ruby, despite its widespread use in web
development and the persistent challenges faced by its developers, has received
little attention in APR research. In this paper, we introduce RAMP, a novel
lightweight framework that formulates program repair as a feedback-driven,
iterative process for Ruby. RAMP employs a team of collaborative agents that
generate targeted tests, reflect on errors, and refine candidate fixes until a
correct solution is found. Unlike prior approaches, RAMP is designed to avoid
reliance on large multilingual repair databases or costly fine-tuning, instead
operating directly on Ruby through lightweight prompting and test-driven
feedback. Evaluation on the XCodeEval benchmark shows that RAMP achieves a
pass@1 of 67% on Ruby, outper-forming prior approaches. RAMP converges quickly
within five iterations, and ablation studies confirm that test generation and
self-reflection are key drivers of its performance. Further analysis shows that
RAMP is particularly effective at repairing wrong answers, compilation errors,
and runtime errors. Our approach provides new insights into multi-agent repair
strategies, and establishes a foundation for extending LLM-based debugging
tools to under-studied languages.

</details>
